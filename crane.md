# The Anatomy of an Origami Crane (Tsuru)

To master the traditional origami crane, you aren't just memorizing steps—you are mastering four foundational concepts of origami design. When you understand these, you can fold almost any traditional model.

## 1. Valley and Mountain Folds (The Binary Code)
Every single crease on the crane is either a **Valley Fold** (folding forward to create a 'V' shape) or a **Mountain Fold** (folding backward to create a ridge). In the first few steps, you use these to map out the grid of the paper.

## 2. The Square Base (Preliminary Base)
By combining diagonal valley folds and horizontal/vertical mountain folds, you collapse the paper into a smaller square that is open at the bottom. This is called the **Square Base**. It is the launchpad for dozens of models.

## 3. The Petal Fold (The Core Challenge)
This is the step that trips most beginners up. You fold the edges inward like a kite, unfold them, open the bottom flap upwards, and guide the sides inward along the creases. This transforms the square into a long diamond shape. This specific move is a **Petal Fold**.

## 4. The Bird Base
Once the petal fold is completed on both sides, you have created the **Bird Base**. This base provides you with four distinct flaps:
*   **Two split legs** at the bottom (which will become the head/neck and the tail).
*   **Two large wings** at the top.

## 5. Inside Reverse Folds (Finishing the Details)
To lift the neck and tail upwards between the layers of the body, you use an **Inside Reverse Fold**. You flip the spine of the paper inside-out to change its direction. You use this exact same fold on a smaller scale to create the beak.
