# The Anatomy of an Origami Crane (Tsuru)

To master the traditional origami crane, you aren't just memorizing steps—you are mastering foundational concepts of origami design. When you understand these, you can fold almost any traditional model.

---

## 1. Valley and Mountain Folds
Every single crease on the crane is either a **Valley Fold** (folding forward to create a 'V' shape) or a **Mountain Fold** (folding backward to create a ridge). In the first few steps, you use these to map out the grid of the paper.

![Valley and Mountain Folds](https://placehold.co/600x400/eeeeee/333333?text=Image:+Valley+and+Mountain+Folds)

---

## 2. The Square Base (Preliminary Base)
By combining diagonal valley folds and horizontal/vertical mountain folds, you collapse the paper into a smaller square that is open at the bottom. This is called the **Square Base**. It is the launchpad for dozens of models.

![Square Base](https://placehold.co/600x400/eeeeee/333333?text=Image:+Square+Base)

---

## 3. The Petal Fold & Bird Base
This is the step that trips most beginners up. You fold the edges inward like a kite, unfold them, open the bottom flap upwards, and guide the sides inward along the creases. This transforms the square into a long diamond shape called the **Bird Base**.

![Bird Base and Petal Fold](https://placehold.co/600x400/eeeeee/333333?text=Image:+Bird+Base+and+Petal+Fold)

---

## 4. Inside Reverse Folds
To lift the neck and tail upwards between the layers of the body, you use an **Inside Reverse Fold**. You flip the spine of the paper inside-out to change its direction. You use this exact same fold on a smaller scale to create the beak.

![Inside Reverse Fold](https://placehold.co/600x400/eeeeee/333333?text=Image:+Inside+Reverse+Fold)
